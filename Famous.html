Women in STEM

Throughout the years there have always been innovative women in STEM, but here are just a few of the marvelous contributions women have made to our society!

Ada Lovelace (1815-1852)

Considered the world's first computer programmer, Ada Lovelace broke gender barriers in the 19th century and made significant contributions to Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine, a forerunner to the modern computer.

Marie Curie (1867-1934)

A pioneering physicist and chemist, Marie Curie was the first woman to receive a Nobel Prize and the first person to win it twice in different fields. Her research led to the discovery of the elements polonium and radium.

Grace Hopper (1906-1992)

An early computer scientist and programmer, Grace Hopper invented the first compiler, a program that translates computer code into machine-readable instructions. She was also a pioneer in developing computer languages and was one of the first programmers to work on the Harvard Mark I computer.

Rosalind Franklin (1920-1958)

A biophysicist and X-ray crystallographer, Rosalind Franklin made significant contributions to the discovery of the structure of DNA, which was largely attributed to her male colleagues. She also contributed to the study of viruses and helped lay the groundwork for modern structural biology.

Katherine Johnson (1918-2020)

A mathematician and computer scientist, Katherine Johnson was instrumental in NASA's early space programs, including the mission that put the first man on the moon. She broke down gender and racial barriers in the STEM field and received numerous honors for her contributions, including the Presidential Medal of Freedom.

Sally Ride (1951-2012)

An astronaut and physicist, Sally Ride was the first American woman in space and the third woman in space overall. She later became a vocal advocate for science education and founded the organization Sally Ride Science to encourage girls to pursue careers in STEM.

Barbara McClintock (1902-1992)

A geneticist and Nobel laureate, Barbara McClintock was a pioneer in the study of genetics and the discovery of transposable genetic elements, also known as jumping genes. She faced significant gender barriers during her career, but her groundbreaking research eventually earned her numerous accolades, including the National Medal of Science.

Mae Jemison (1956-present)

An astronaut, physician, and engineer, Mae Jemison was the first African American woman in space. She also worked as a doctor in Africa and founded The Jemison Group, a technology and consulting firm focused on improving quality of life through science and technology.

Chien-Shiung Wu (1912-1997)

A physicist and experimentalist, Chien-Shiung Wu made significant contributions to the study of nuclear physics and beta decay. She also worked on the Manhattan Project during World War II and broke down gender barriers in the STEM field, paving the way for future generations of women scientists.
